Healthy Cooking Guide

Healthy cooking is about making nutritious, delicious meals without sacrificing flavor. This guide covers the fundamentals of meal prep, understanding macronutrients, essential cooking techniques, and smart ingredient choices.

Understanding Macronutrients
Every meal should balance three macronutrients. Proteins (chicken, fish, eggs, legumes, tofu) repair and build muscle tissue and keep you satiated. Aim for 0.7–1g of protein per pound of body weight per day. Carbohydrates (whole grains, sweet potatoes, oats, fruits, vegetables) are the body's primary energy source — choose complex carbs over refined ones for sustained energy. Healthy fats (avocado, olive oil, nuts, seeds, fatty fish) support brain function and hormone production. Fat is not the enemy; trans fats found in processed foods are what to avoid.

Meal Prep Basics
Meal prepping on Sundays saves time, reduces food waste, and prevents impulsive unhealthy choices during the week. Start by choosing 2–3 proteins, 2–3 complex carbs, and 4–5 vegetables. Cook proteins in bulk using an oven, air fryer, or stovetop. Roast a large sheet pan of mixed vegetables with olive oil, salt, and pepper at 200°C for 25 minutes. Cook a big pot of grains (brown rice, quinoa, or farro). Store everything in airtight glass containers and refrigerate for up to 4 days.

Healthy Cooking Methods
Steaming preserves the most nutrients and requires no oil. Stir-frying over high heat with minimal oil is fast and retains texture. Baking and roasting enhance flavor through caramelization without deep frying. Poaching is ideal for eggs and delicate proteins like fish. Avoid deep frying and excessive butter-based sautéing.

Ingredient Substitutions
Replace white rice with cauliflower rice to cut calories and add fiber. Use Greek yogurt instead of sour cream for the same creaminess with more protein. Swap regular pasta for zucchini noodles or chickpea pasta for a lower-carb, higher-protein option. Use mashed banana or unsweetened applesauce instead of sugar in baked goods. Replace heavy cream with coconut milk in curries and soups. Use lettuce wraps instead of tortillas for tacos.

Sample Weekly Meal Ideas
Breakfast: overnight oats with chia seeds, almond milk, and berries; or scrambled eggs with spinach and whole-grain toast.
Lunch: quinoa bowl with grilled chicken, roasted chickpeas, cucumber, tomato, and tahini dressing.
Dinner: baked salmon with sweet potato mash and steamed broccoli; or a lentil and vegetable stew with brown rice.
Snacks: apple slices with almond butter; Greek yogurt with walnuts; hummus with carrot sticks.

Seasoning Without Salt
Excessive sodium raises blood pressure. Flavor food with fresh herbs (basil, cilantro, parsley), spices (cumin, turmeric, smoked paprika, chili), citrus zest and juice, garlic, ginger, and balsamic vinegar. Build flavor layers by toasting dry spices in a pan before adding wet ingredients.

Reading Labels
When buying packaged food, check the ingredients list first — shorter is better. Look for added sugars (hidden as corn syrup, dextrose, fructose). Compare sodium per 100g rather than per serving. Choose products with whole food ingredients in the top three positions. Ignore front-of-pack marketing claims and go straight to the nutrition panel.

Hydration
Drinking enough water supports digestion, metabolism, and energy levels. Aim for 2–3 liters per day. Herbal teas count toward fluid intake. Limit sugary drinks, energy drinks, and alcohol, which add empty calories and impair nutrient absorption.

Kitchen Essentials
Keep your kitchen stocked with: extra-virgin olive oil, canned tomatoes, dried lentils and beans, whole grains, low-sodium broth, apple cider vinegar, garlic, onions, frozen vegetables, eggs, and natural nut butters. With these staples you can build a healthy meal in under 30 minutes.
