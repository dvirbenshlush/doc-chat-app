Fitness and Exercise Guide

Regular exercise improves cardiovascular health, builds strength, boosts mood, and extends lifespan. This guide provides a practical framework for building a sustainable fitness routine, whether you are a complete beginner or returning after a break.

Types of Exercise
Cardiovascular exercise (cardio) raises your heart rate and improves heart and lung efficiency. Examples include running, cycling, swimming, rowing, and jump rope. Aim for 150 minutes of moderate-intensity cardio (brisk walk) or 75 minutes of vigorous cardio (running) per week according to WHO guidelines. Strength training builds and preserves muscle mass, increases bone density, and raises your resting metabolic rate. It includes free weights, resistance machines, bodyweight exercises, and resistance bands. Train each muscle group 2–3 times per week with at least 48 hours of rest between sessions for the same group. Flexibility and mobility work (stretching, yoga, Pilates) improve range of motion, reduce injury risk, and aid recovery.

Creating a Weekly Plan
A balanced beginner schedule might look like: Monday — full-body strength training; Tuesday — 30-minute jog or cycle; Wednesday — rest or light yoga; Thursday — full-body strength; Friday — 30-minute swim or HIIT; Saturday — active recovery walk; Sunday — complete rest. As you progress, split strength training into upper/lower body or push/pull/legs to allow greater training volume.

Warm-Up Routine
Never skip a warm-up. Spend 5–10 minutes raising body temperature and preparing joints before any intense session. A good warm-up includes 3–5 minutes of light cardio (jumping jacks, brisk walk), followed by dynamic stretches: leg swings, arm circles, hip circles, high knees, and bodyweight squats. Dynamic stretching (movement-based) is more effective pre-workout than static stretching (holding), which is better saved for after exercise.

Core Strength Training Exercises
For beginners, master these foundational movements: squat (targets quads, glutes, hamstrings), hinge/deadlift (hamstrings, glutes, lower back), push-up (chest, shoulders, triceps), row (back, biceps), overhead press (shoulders, triceps), and plank (core stability). Start with bodyweight or light resistance, focus on form before adding load, and progress gradually by adding weight, reps, or sets over time.

HIIT Training
High-Intensity Interval Training alternates short bursts of maximal effort (20–40 seconds) with brief recovery periods (10–20 seconds). A typical session lasts 15–25 minutes but produces similar cardiovascular adaptations to 45 minutes of steady-state cardio. Examples: 8 rounds of 20 seconds sprinting + 10 seconds rest (Tabata protocol); or 30 seconds burpees, 30 seconds mountain climbers, 30 seconds jump squats, 30 seconds rest, repeated 4 times. Limit HIIT to 2–3 sessions per week due to its high recovery demand.

Cool-Down and Stretching
Spend 5–10 minutes cooling down after intense sessions. Walk slowly for 3–5 minutes to gradually lower heart rate, then perform static stretches holding each for 20–30 seconds: hamstring stretch, quadriceps stretch, hip flexor lunge, chest opener, shoulder cross-body stretch, and child's pose for the lower back. This improves flexibility over time and reduces next-day soreness.

Nutrition for Exercise
Eat a small carbohydrate-rich meal 1–2 hours before training for energy (banana, oats, toast). After training, consume protein and carbohydrates within 45 minutes to maximize muscle repair and glycogen replenishment (protein shake with a banana, Greek yogurt, or chicken with rice). Stay well-hydrated — drink 500ml of water 2 hours before exercise and sip 150–250ml every 15–20 minutes during activity. Electrolytes (sodium, potassium, magnesium) become important during sessions exceeding 60 minutes.

Recovery and Rest
Muscle growth occurs during rest, not during exercise. Aim for 7–9 hours of sleep per night, as this is when growth hormone is released and tissue repair peaks. Active recovery (light walking, swimming, or yoga on rest days) increases blood flow and clears metabolic waste products without adding training stress. Foam rolling (self-myofascial release) helps release muscle tightness and reduce soreness.

Tracking Progress
Keep a training log — record exercises, sets, reps, and weights. Take progress photos every 4 weeks under consistent lighting. Measure strength improvements (e.g., how much weight you can lift) and cardiovascular performance (e.g., resting heart rate, time to run 5km) rather than focusing only on body weight, which fluctuates daily. Plateaus are normal — when progress stalls, change training variables: increase weight, add sets, shorten rest periods, or switch to a new exercise variation.
